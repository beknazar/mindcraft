## The 5 Key Principles of Growth Loops

### 1. Loops > Funnels
A funnel goes: Awareness → Interest → Decision → Action. It's linear. Users leak at every step, and you need to keep pouring new users in at the top.

A loop goes: User creates → Output is visible → New user discovers → New user creates → ...

**The difference:** Funnels require constant input. Loops are self-reinforcing. Every user going through the loop creates the potential for more users.

### 2. Every Product Has a Potential Loop
It might not be obvious, but it's there. Ask: "What does my user create or do that could be visible to non-users?"
- Notion: public pages and templates
- Figma: shared design files
- Calendly: meeting links sent to non-users
- Slack: inviting teammates

### 3. The Loop Must Be Natural
Forced virality (pop-ups asking "invite 3 friends!") doesn't work. The sharing has to be a natural part of using the product. Calendly doesn't ask you to share — sharing IS the product.

### 4. Free Users Power the Loop
This is why freemium exists. Free users create the content, send the invites, generate the social proof. They're not freeloaders — they're your distribution engine.

### 5. Measure Loop Efficiency, Not Just Conversion
Track: for every 100 users who enter the loop, how many new users does it produce? If it's 10, your loop has a 10% efficiency. Anything above 0% means your growth compounds.

## Framework: How to Build a Growth Loop

**Step 1: Identify the user action** — What do users DO in your product?

**Step 2: Find the output** — What artifact does that action create? (A page, a link, a file, a result)

**Step 3: Make it visible** — How can non-users see that output? (Public pages, shared links, social posts, emails)

**Step 4: Create a call to action** — When non-users see the output, what makes them want to try the product? ("Made with X", "Create your own", "Sign up to edit")

**Step 5: Measure and optimize** — Track loop efficiency. Optimize the weakest step.

---

## Common Mistakes
1. Building a loop that requires extra effort from users (they won't do it)
2. Optimizing the top of the funnel when the loop is broken
3. Paying for acquisition when you could invest in making the loop work
4. Measuring vanity metrics instead of loop efficiency
