# Paul Graham — Anti-Patterns (What He Warns Against)

## 1. Playing Startup
Building a company that looks like a startup (office, logo, business cards, pitch deck) but isn't actually making something people want. All theater, no substance. "If you do everything the way the average startup does it, you should expect average performance."

## 2. The Tar Pit Idea
Ideas that seem interesting and attract lots of founders but are actually very hard to monetize. Social apps for college students, recommendation engines, "Yelp for X." They're craters — wide appeal, shallow engagement.

## 3. Thinking Up Ideas (Instead of Noticing Them)
"The verb you want to be using with respect to startup ideas is not 'think up' but 'notice.'" Sitting in a room brainstorming startup ideas produces bad ideas. Living in the future and noticing what's missing produces good ones.

## 4. Premature Scaling
Hiring, raising money, and building infrastructure before finding product-market fit. You're optimizing a machine that doesn't work yet. "What you should fear is not the established players, but other startups you don't know exist yet."

## 5. Hiring Professional Fakers (Manager Mode)
From "Founder Mode" (2024): Following the conventional advice to "hire good people and give them room" often means hiring people who are great at appearing competent. Stay in founder mode — stay involved in the details that matter.

## 6. Building in Stealth
Working in secret for months/years before showing anyone. Launch as soon as you have a "quantum of utility." "It's easy to see how little launches matter."

## 7. Obstinacy Disguised as Persistence
Being attached to your idea about how to reach the goal, rather than the goal itself. "The persistent are attached to the goal. The obstinate are attached to their ideas about how to reach it." If your eyes glaze over when someone disagrees with your approach, you're obstinate, not persistent.

## 8. Competing on Features
Adding more features instead of making core features better. Users don't want more options — they want the main thing to work really well.

## 9. Fundraising as Achievement
Treating fundraising as a milestone rather than a means. "When you're fundraising, be fundraising. When you're not, don't." Some founders get addicted to the fundraising high and never build anything.

## 10. Not Writing, Not Thinking
From "Writes and Write-Nots" (2025): "If you're thinking without writing, you only think you're thinking." Founders who don't write clearly don't think clearly. Society will split into "thinks and think-nots."

## 11. Solving the Wrong Problem
"You can either build something a large number of people want a small amount, or something a small number of people want a large amount." Most failed startups chose the crater. Build the well.

## 12. Ignoring the Schlep
Great startup ideas hiding in plain sight because they seem tedious or unpleasant. "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." Stripe was invisible to hackers for a decade because payments seemed like a nightmare.
