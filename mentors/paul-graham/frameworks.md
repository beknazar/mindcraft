# Paul Graham — Decision Frameworks

## The Startup Ideas Filter
From "How to Get Startup Ideas" — the three traits of the best startup ideas:
1. **Something the founders themselves want** — You understand the problem viscerally
2. **Something they themselves can build** — You don't need permission or resources
3. **Something few others realize are worth doing** — The competition hasn't noticed yet

If your idea has all three, you're in the sweet spot. Most founders fail by "solving problems no one has."

## The Schlep Filter
When evaluating ideas, ask: "Am I avoiding this because it's actually bad, or because it seems tedious?" Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps. The schlep IS the moat. Stripe existed as an opportunity for a decade because hackers' unconscious minds shrank from the complications.

**How to use it:** "Schleps should be dealt with the same way you'd deal with a cold swimming pool: just jump in."

## Default Alive / Default Dead
Calculate: at your current revenue growth rate and burn rate, do you reach profitability before running out of money?
- **Default alive:** You'll make it even if you stop fundraising. You have leverage.
- **Default dead:** You'll run out of money. Act NOW. Cut costs, increase growth, or both.
- Most founders are default dead and in denial about it.

## The Well vs The Crater
From "How to Get Startup Ideas":
- **The crater:** Build something a large number of people want a small amount (wide and shallow — dangerous)
- **The well:** Build something a small number of people want a large amount (narrow and deep — this is what you want)

Start with the well. You can always make it wider later. You can't make a crater deeper.

## Founder Mode vs Manager Mode
From the September 2024 essay:
- **Manager mode:** Hire good people, give them room. Sounds right but often means "hire professional fakers."
- **Founder mode:** Stay involved in details that matter. Skip levels. Run retreats for the 100 most important people regardless of org chart (Jobs at Apple).

The conventional wisdom of "hire and delegate" fails because it assumes you can always identify good people from the outside. Founders who stay engaged catch problems managers miss.

## The Quantum of Utility Test
When to launch: as soon as there's ONE person in the world who would be glad you launched. Not "it's ready." Not "it's polished." Just: does it help someone?

## The Ramen Profitable Threshold
Get to the point where revenue covers founders' living expenses. This changes everything:
- You're default alive
- You have leverage in fundraising
- You can take time to find the right thing
- You're not desperate

## The Four Steps to Great Work
From "How to Do Great Work" (2023):
1. **Choose a field** — What are you excessively curious about?
2. **Learn enough to reach the frontier** — Get to the point where you know what's been done
3. **Notice gaps** — What's missing? What's wrong? What could exist but doesn't?
4. **Explore promising gaps** — Original ideas come from trying to build or understand something slightly too difficult

## Organic vs Paid Growth Signal
If your users are coming from organic/word-of-mouth, you probably have something. If you're only growing via paid acquisition, you probably don't. Organic growth is the signal that people actually want what you're building.

## Persistence vs Obstinacy Check
From "The Right Kind of Stubborn" (2024):
- **Persistent:** Attached to the goal. "Never more engaged than when you disagree with them."
- **Obstinate:** Attached to their ideas about how to reach it. "Eyes glaze over" when challenged.

Five components of persistence: energy, imagination, resilience, good judgment, focus on a goal.

## The Formidable Founder Test
Can PG tell in YC interviews if people truly understand their idea? Yes — versus those who just have "reading-the-newspaper level of understanding." The test: "If a group of founders seemed impressive enough, I'd fund them with no idea."
