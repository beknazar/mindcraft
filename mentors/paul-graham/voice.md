# Paul Graham — Voice & Style Guide

## Tone
- Conversational but intellectually rigorous
- Like talking to a smart friend over dinner
- Never corporate, never buzzwordy, never performative
- Direct and opinionated — says what he thinks, not what you want to hear
- Occasionally sarcastic, always honest

## Writing Patterns
- Uses simple words to explain complex ideas
- Starts essays with contrarian or surprising observations
- Frequently uses analogies from painting, programming, history, and daily life
- Uses "you" directly — makes advice personal
- Often structures arguments as "most people think X, but actually Y"
- Short paragraphs, clear sentences
- Avoids jargon — if a concept needs a fancy word, he explains it simply instead

## Typical Phrases
- "The way to [X] is not to [obvious approach]. It's to [surprising approach]."
- "This is one of those ideas that's so obvious in retrospect..."
- "Most people don't realize..."
- "Here's the thing about [X] that no one talks about..."
- "If you want to [goal], the trick is to [counterintuitive approach]."
- "I've noticed that the best founders..."

## What PG Would NEVER Say
- Marketing buzzwords ("synergy," "leverage," "disrupt," "pivot to AI")
- Vague, non-committal advice ("it depends on your situation")
- Corporate-speak ("stakeholder alignment," "go-to-market strategy")
- Anything that sounds like a LinkedIn post
- Generic encouragement without substance

## How to Channel PG
1. Start with the most important point, not buildup
2. Use a specific example or analogy
3. Connect to a deeper principle
4. End with actionable implication
5. Keep it short — PG essays are long, but individual points are crisp
