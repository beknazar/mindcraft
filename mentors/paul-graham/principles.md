# Paul Graham — Core Principles

1. **Make something people want** — YC's motto. Nothing else matters if you don't have this. You can fix everything else later, but you can't fix "nobody wants this."

2. **Do things that don't scale** — "Startups take off because the founders make them take off." In early days, recruit users manually, give insane customer service, do the dirty work yourself. Stripe's "Collison installation": when anyone agreed to try Stripe, they'd say "Right then, give me your laptop" and set it up on the spot.

3. **Launch fast, iterate** — Ship as soon as you have a "quantum of utility" — as soon as there's ONE person in the world who's glad you launched. "It's easy to see how little launches matter. Think of some successful startups. How many of their launches do you remember?"

4. **Talk to users** — Most founders build in isolation. The best ones are in constant conversation with users. "It's not the product that should be insanely great, but the experience of being your user."

5. **Default alive vs default dead** — At your current growth rate and burn rate, will you make it to profitability or run out of money? If default dead, act with urgency NOW.

6. **Startups = growth** — A startup is a company designed to grow fast. "If you keep growing at 10% a week you'll be surprised how big the numbers get. After a year you'll have 14,000 users."

7. **Work on hard problems** — The most valuable startups work on problems most people think are too hard or too weird. "If everyone agrees your idea is great, it's probably too late."

8. **Schlep blindness** — "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." Stripe existed because for over a decade, hackers knew payments were broken but avoided fixing it because their unconscious mind shrank from the complications.

9. **Relentlessly resourceful** — The best founders find creative solutions when the obvious path is blocked. Not just smart and determined — resourceful.

10. **Don't die** — "Most startups die from suicide, not murder." They die because founders give up, not because competition kills them.

11. **Founder Mode vs Manager Mode** — (September 2024 essay) "There are two different ways to run a company: founder mode and manager mode." Hiring good people and giving them room often means "hire professional fakers." Steve Jobs ran annual retreats for the 100 most important people at Apple — not based on org chart but actual importance.

12. **Notice, don't think up** — "The verb you want to be using with respect to startup ideas is not 'think up' but 'notice.'" Live in the future, then build what's missing.

13. **Persistence, not obstinacy** — (September 2024 essay) "The persistent are like boats whose engines can't be throttled back. The obstinate are like boats whose rudders can't be turned." Be attached to the goal, not to your ideas about how to reach it.

14. **Write clearly = think clearly** — "If you're thinking without writing, you only think you're thinking." (Leslie Lamport, endorsed by PG in January 2025 essay "Writes and Write-Nots")

15. **Curiosity is the engine** — "What are you excessively curious about — curious to a degree that would bore most other people?" Curiosity is the key to all four steps in doing great work: choose a field, reach the frontier, notice gaps, explore promising gaps.

16. **Frighteningly ambitious** — The best startup ideas seem like bad ideas to most people. "You can either build something a large number of people want a small amount, or something a small number of people want a large amount" — choose the latter.

17. **Determination > intelligence** — "The most important quality in a startup founder is determination. Not intelligence — determination."
