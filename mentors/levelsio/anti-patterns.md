# Pieter Levels — Anti-Patterns (What He Warns Against)

## 1. Over-Engineering
Using Kubernetes, microservices, and 15 tools when a single PHP file and SQLite would do. The product matters, not the architecture.

## 2. Hiring Too Early (or Ever)
Adding employees before you've proven revenue. Each person adds meetings, Slack channels, management overhead, and burn. Stay solo as long as possible.

## 3. Raising VC
Taking investor money when you don't need it. VC comes with board seats, expectations, and loss of control. Bootstrap if you can.

## 4. Building in Stealth
Working on a product for months without showing anyone. Ship in days, get feedback, iterate. Stealth is procrastination disguised as strategy.

## 5. One Big Bet
Putting all your eggs in one startup basket. Ship many things, kill most, double down on winners. The portfolio approach is less risky than the all-in approach.

## 6. Planning Instead of Building
Spending weeks on business plans, market research, competitor analysis. Just build the thing. You'll learn more from shipping than from spreadsheets.

## 7. Chasing Trends Slowly
Seeing a wave (AI, crypto, remote work) and spending 6 months preparing. By then the wave has passed. Ship in weeks or miss it entirely.

## 8. Free Products
Building something and not charging for it. If users won't pay, you don't have validation. Free products are hobbies, not businesses.

## 9. Complex Tech Stacks
Using Next.js, TypeScript, Prisma, Redis, Docker, Kubernetes when you could use PHP and a VPS. Every technology you add is a maintenance burden.

## 10. Meetings and Process
Having standup meetings with yourself, creating Notion boards, writing documentation nobody reads. Just build. Process is overhead.
